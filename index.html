President's Message

TBFC – 2021-2022 Season Begins!

Well, we did it. After a 15 month absence, we were able to meet in person at USF for our first meeting of the 2021-2022 season. While we are all a little out of practice, it seemed to go well and we had about seventy people in attendance. Everyone enjoyed the presentation on Eocene mammals by Dr. Jonathan Bloch. We gave out some prizes and even awarded a few plaques to folks for service in TBFC.

I want to thank Dr. Sinibaldi for his hard work over the decades. He is a well deserving recipient of the Marvin Preston Club Service Award. Also receiving an award was Eileen Durkin for her gracious help during COVID in keeping TBFC’s presence online and her invaluable assistance in hosting our virtual meetings on YouTube live. Thank you both. I too was honored as a recipient of the Marvin Preston Club Service Award. I am thankful for the award and will tell you all that the work I’ve done over the past decades for TBFC has been nothing but rewarding. My first meeting as president of TBFC was just three days before the 9/11 attacks. All of our speakers for the remainder of 2001 canceled on us. Later we were displaced from USF because of the al-Arian terrorist threat. Between Barbara Fite, my wife Seina, and the rest of the Board, we pulled it all together. We found speakers, found a place to meet, and eventually talked our way back into USF.

While the Marvin Preston Club Service Award thanks me for my service, in turn I thank all of you and TBFC. As I say in my article here at the close of each season, “Thanks for the adventure TBFC!”

I need to thank Dr. Jonathan Bloch for making the long drive to Tampa to meet with us. Everyone gets excited when we get folks from the Florida Museum of Natural History to speak, and for good reason. Lots of members brought along fossils to show off and have identified by Dr. Bloch, heck Dave Hoffman brought along a whole box of cool fossils.

Field Trips

Despite Marlin Swikert’s best efforts, all of the mines we hunt will remain closed to visitors through the end of 2021. On the bright side, Fred Hendershot predicts that all of the rains and flooding should make for a great fossil hunting season in the rivers when the water levels finally do subside. We have also added a Venice shark tooth dive to our field trips on October 16. If it’s successful, we’ll make that a regular trip on our schedule. Thanks to member Doug Woodring and Top 2 Bottom Charters for helping make the dive trip possible.

Please keep a watch out for any changes that COVID could cause with our scheduled events.

Read the Chronicles each month (Members Only), check the calendar here on the TBFC website, and follow us on Facebook. Also, please be very cautious while venturing out into the rivers and creeks. At the time of this writing we are seeing flood stage conditions in most waterways. There’s nothing in those rivers worth dying for.

A few things for the remainder of 2021...

We will meet in November for our annual auction. Attendance in the past for this event has been low, despite some great auction items (and good company). We really need the boost for scholarship revenue, so I’d like to invite everyone to show up for a good time. If you have auction items to donate, please bring them along, or contact me in advance if you need someone to pick them up. I’d really appreciate any sup- port we can get for the auction.

There will be no meeting in December. Not necessarily by choice, but more of a scheduling conflict with USF. Hopefully this won’t be a recurring problem. Come January we will need to hit the ground running. Despite continuing challenges with COVID, FossilFest 2022 is a GO! The Peace River Adventure is a GO! I say both with enthusiasm... unless COVID shuts down the world again.

With positive thoughts on all of our futures, I hope to see you all at the October meeting. To all of you kids, “BOO”, have a great Halloween!

Good Hunting!

Mike Searle

Oct. 02, 2021
Guest Speaker: David Letasi - Terror Pigs and Walking Whales!

7PM IN-PERSON CLUB MEETING at the USF Behavioral Science Room #103
We will also try to be Live on YouTube but the live stream quality is not guarnteed and the stream might not even work! But we will try! (Note: some speakers may not want to be filmed).

David Letasi has been interested in fossils and pre-historic times since he was eight years old and thus has had many years’ experience searching for fossils all over North America and Asia. By age twelve, he had discovered three archeological sites that are recorded in the Michigan State Archeological Site Survey. In addition to dinosaurs, Letasi has studied prehistoric man, ancient world history, and archeology.

After serving in the Navy, he decided to travel around the United States to collect specimens from well-known fossil sites in Wyoming, Nebraska, South Dakota, and Florida, as well as Canada and several countries in Asia. After he married his wife, Suzanne, in 1972 she began to travel with him. In 1976 they moved to Florida where it was possible to collect saber-tooth cat fossils, his main field of interest.

Dave worked for Great Explorations Museum in St Petersburg and assumed a number of duties, including designing and curating exhibits and later worked at the Museum of Science and Industry (MOSI) in Tampa. While at MOSI, he led cave explorations for children and adults to the Dames Cave in the Withlacoochee State Foest, as well as fossil-collecting trips to mining sites in Polk County. Since retiring from MOSI in 2007, he has been active in projects with the Historic Hernando Preservation Society.

His lengthy résumé includes being the lead archaeological project coordinator with the Historic Hernando County Preservation Society, the Seminole Tribe of Florida, and the Gulf Archaeology Research Institute of Gainesville. Some interesting projects he’s worked on include searching for the eighteenth-century capital of the Seminole Nation and the exploration of the Bayport Civil War shipwreck site.

Dave’s presentation will discuss “Terror Pigs and Walking Whales", the convergent relationship between the Cetacea and primitive Artiodactals. This lecture will follow the research and paleontological history of the discovery of vertebrate fossils of early whales and their cousins the Entelodonts.
